BIOLOGICAL EVOLUTIONS

Biological evolutions started when living cells started to appear forms the core part of the Darwin's theory of the evolutions, Microorganisms evolve very fast because they reproduce quickly, whereas larger animals evolve slowly since their life span is long Lamarck earlier proposed that organisms evolved by using or not using organs, but this idea was rejected because acquired characters cannot be inherited. Evolution is therefore understood as a gradual process driven by inherited variations.

MECHANISM OF EVOLUTION

What is the origin of this variation and how does speciation occur? Even though Mendel had talked of inheritable 'factors' influencing phenotype, Darwin either ignored these observations or kept silence. In the first decade of twentieth century, Hugo Devries based on his work on evening primrose brought forth the idea of mutations-large difference arising suddenly in a population. He believed that it is mutation which causes evolution and not the minor variations (heritable) that Darwin talked about. Mutations are random and directionless while Darwinian variations are small and directional. Evolution for Darwin was gradual while Devries believed mutation caused speciation and hence called it saltation (single step large mutation). Studies in population genetics, later, brought out some clarity.

HARDY-WEINBERG PRINCIPLE

The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that the allele frequencies in a large, randomly mating population remain constant from generation to generation if no evolutionary forces act on it. This stable condition is called genetic equilibrium. If the frequencies deviate from expected mathematical values, evolution is taking place. The principle uses the formula p² + 2pq + q² = 1 to represent the frequencies of different genotypes. Factors such as mutation, gene flow, genetic drift, recombination and natural selection disturb this equilibrium and cause evolutionary change.

A BRIEF ACCOUNT OF EVOLUTION

Life on earth began around 2000 million years ago with simple cells. Over time, single celled organisms gave rise to multicellular forms. About 500 million years ago, invertebrates appeared, followed by jawless fish and then amphibians. Plants invaded land before animals. Lobefin fish evolved into the first amphibians, which later gave rise to reptiles. Reptiles dominated the earth for millions of years, including the dinosaurs. Around 65 million years ago, dinosaurs disappeared due to unknown reasons. Mammals, which were small earlier, began to dominate and diversified into many forms, including those that returned to water like whales and dolphins. Evolution continued through various geological periods, leading to the diversity we see today.
